One hundred oxen

...is the number of animals slaughtered and sacrificed to the gods on very special occasions. The name for it is 'hecatomb (ἑκατόμβη)' which literally means '100 oxen'. Such a huge sacrifice was meant to impress both gods and men with the richness of it and it was performed only rarely. 'Slaughter' is the keyword here: this website is about the Iliad and the Iliad is, or rather describes, an enormous slaughter. Anyone who tried to read it knows it well: the poem seems an endless sequence of very formalized sword and spear duels, interspersed with dialog scenes by gods and men. Why read it? This website hopes to give a beginning of an answer.

100 oxen

is also the name of this website. I chose it because it fits the Iliad very well and because of a joke Homer makes in book 6 (Il 6.119-): here the great hero Diomedes meets an equally great warrior from the Trojan side, Glaucos, on the battlefield. Before they fight, they do the usual boasting of their prowess and lineage and it turns out that their families have old ties. They choose not to fight each other and exchange armor instead (the enemy armor that you gain in battle is a measure of your worth as a warrior) . Glaucos has golden  armor, a sure sign of superhero status, and Homer comments: Glaucos must have lost his wits because he exchanged armor worth a hundred oxen, for a bronze one worth only nine (ἑκατόμβοι᾽ ἐννεαβοίων = a hundred oxen for nine). This is an ironical comment on Diomedes' too-good-to-be-true way of becoming a superhero - the likelihood of making a deal like that. Because of his likeness to and contrast with Achilles I called the first part of the structure after him: 'the immortal hero'. This phrase 'a hundred oxen for nine' also means: 'a hecatomb for nine oxen' which is exactly the whole Iliad in 2 words. The Iliad is like a hecatomb, the nine oxen are like Achilles plus Agamemnon's council : Agamemnon and Menelaos (the old king, the young king), Nestor and Odysseus (the old Councillor, the young(er) one), the two Aiantes (the greater, the lesser), Idomeneus and Diomedes (the old hero, the young one). These eight are the main Greek actors in the story; they are also the eight little birdies eaten, plus Achilles the mother-bird, by the snake in Kalchas' prophecy as told by Odysseus in Il 2.308-.

The above paragraph is an example of how I propose to tackle the Iliad: the poem is to be understood by likeness. I shall happily ignore modern theories about the futility of this exercise and proceed with an analysis of the Iliad governed by simple likenesses of 3 kinds: internal, external and reflexive.

  1. Internal: an example of the Homerum ex Homero principle. I have tried to analyze the poem 'bottom-up' by taking short sequences of hexameters and organize them by topic, trying to formulate 'what it is about' for every group and gathering them recursively into larger parts based on likeness and contrast, looking for symmetry and ring-composition and forming a hierarchical structure. The result is on the main page, where I hope to demonstrate this internal poetical structure of the Iliad, a 'structure of ideas'. This structure is probably not there for the benefit of the audience but functions as a sort of mnemonic structure which helps the poet to organize and remember his material. More on the theory behind it here. It has to be said, the determination 'what it is about' is naturally not separable from interpretation, therefore subjective. But because the parts of the structure are related by likeness and/or contrast, this gives us a clue what the poet is talking about in his own mind. An example is the correspondence Diomedes - Achilles. It tells us that the poet is contrasting these two heroes and that is an important pointer for the interpretation of the whole poem.
  2. External: On the simplest level: the myriad simple metaphors, similes and comparisons which make up a large part of Homeric language. I shall call them all 'likeness' to avoid theoretical complications which are of no interest here. The intention of this language-use is to create a "semi-hidden" level of meaning, apart from the literal. More about this plus a dictionary of the most important ones here.
    On the largest level,  there is a correspondence between the situation of the Achaean army in the Iliad  with the political situation in Ionia in the 8-7th century.
  3. Reflexive: looking at the possibility that the Iliad and especially the Odyssey have a lot to say about themselves, their public and their poet(s). The reflexivity, if accepted, would make a strong case for a unitarian view but there are other possibilities, too.

Before beginning, let me go through the set of unprovables, the axioms that determine the rules of interpretation in this Homeric cosmos:

This means that every line, every scene is in the service of this and we will not allow for digressions, interpolations etc. which go outside this unity. I know that this can never be 100% true as 1) it is impossible, in view of the complicated history of the poem, to think that we possess the exact text that the poet used to sing (if there ever was such a thing) and 2) given the huge cultural distance between us and them, we can never be sure of any interpretation. But we should start from the assumption that everything in it is relevant and intentional. 
It must be clear that the Iliad is not a novel. It is not about character, it is about action (Aristotle said this). Action is not determined by individual character but by position. With 'position' I mean the sum of the forces acting upon a person when he/she is making a decision, such as social status and expectations of others but also, in Homeric terms, a choice 'which god to obey'. About the gods here. I do not mean to say that the characters in the poem are only types. Homer is too good a poet for that. We could say they are not without character, but individual character is not the driving force of their actions. The gods are.

This axiom says that the Iliad (also) refers to a historical reality outside itself and outside the world of myth. We may not know very much about it, but we must apply all of our historical and archaeological knowledge to aid the interpretation of this poem. The consensus seems to be for a date (of the Iliad) in the late 8th-early 7th century so we will have to look for facts from that time at the latest.
We presume that a poet in the 8-7th c. composed one or two poems to comment on events and developments in his own world and that he used and adapted well-known stories from the world of myth to express his ideas, and also to hide them. This was in the archaic Greek world and earlier (non-literate societies) a common use of myth. One example of this use is the political use of charter-myth.

On one level, this poet really is a master-manipulator of pathos. We must imagine his public as mainly young, male, aristocratic (all little Achillesses), eager for honor and glory and very much pro-Greek. This is the public the literal level of meaning is aimed at: a public wildly enthusiastic about the very battle scenes that we find so boring. He could control their pity and fear (another Aristotle reference here) as well as other emotions like no other poet could. 'Learning by pathos' is the keyword here.
On another rhetorical level, it is a sharp criticism of Ionian politics of his day. This is aimed at 'kings and prominent people' (ref. Il 2.188-207), generally those who understand allusion and irony. For this audience, the Iliad is meant to function as a speculum regis, a king's mirror. It becomes the 'ethical constitution' of the city-state, Sparta in its early days is probably the best example of that.

They belonging to that poem and to that poet only. This view of the gods is not characteristic of the Greeks in general, must have been controversial even in Homer's day and is much normalized in the Odyssey. The divine-human interaction and especially the so-called 'double determination' of human actions are created by Homer to explain something to us concerning character, fate and justice. The most important background story for this is the 'Judgement of Paris', the prelude to the Trojan War.
